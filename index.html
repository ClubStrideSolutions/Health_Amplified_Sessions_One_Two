The 5 Whys Framework 30 mins

  • Introduction to root cause analysis
  • Simple 5 Whys demonstration
  • Guided practice exercise
+

Key Terms

Root Cause Analysis: A method that identifies the underlying source of a problem rather than treating symptoms.

5 Whys Technique: A simple technique of asking "why" multiple times to dig deeper into an issue.

Discussion Points

  • Why do we often address symptoms instead of underlying causes?
  • How can asking "why" help us understand youth health issues better?

Activity

Part 1 (10 mins): Facilitator demonstrates the 5 Whys technique using a simple example:

  • Problem: "Students aren't accessing free STI testing at the health center."
  • Why? "They don't know about it."
  • Why? "The information isn't reaching them."
  • Why? "Announcements are only posted in certain locations."
  • Why? "There's a limited outreach strategy."
  • Why? "Youth input wasn't included in communication planning."

Part 2 (15 mins): In pairs, practice using the 5 Whys:

  1. Choose ONE simple problem from the following list:
    • Youth skipping meals
    • Low attendance at health workshops
    • Difficulty accessing online resources
  2. Ask "why" at least 3-5 times
  3. Write your answers on the provided worksheet

Part 3 (5 mins): Volunteers share what they discovered.

Reflection Questions

  • What surprised you about the causes you identified?
  • How might this technique help with your project planning?

SMART Goal Framework 30 mins

  • Basic goal-setting principles
  • Understanding SMART components
  • Practice with examples
+

Key Terms

SMART Goals: Goals that are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

Specific: Clearly defines what will be accomplished.

Measurable: Includes criteria for tracking progress.

Achievable: Realistic given your resources and constraints.

Relevant: Aligned with your broader goals and needs.

Time-bound: Has a clear deadline.

Discussion Points

  • Why do vague goals often fail?
  • How can specific metrics help us track progress?

Activity

Part 1 (10 mins): Facilitator introduces SMART goal components with examples:

  • Not SMART: "Increase youth awareness about health resources."
  • SMART: "Create 10 resource cards about local health services and distribute to at least 50 students by May 31, 2025."

Part 2 (15 mins): In pairs, transform these vague goals into SMART goals:

  1. "Help students learn about nutrition."
  2. "Make a social media campaign about mental health."
  3. "Improve access to transportation."

Part 3 (5 mins): Share your best example with the group.

Reflection Questions

  • Which SMART component was hardest to define?
  • How might you use SMART goals in your project planning?

KPI Development for Public Health 40 mins

  • Identifying meaningful indicators
  • Process vs. outcome measures
  • Creating KPI dashboards
+

Key Terms

Key Performance Indicator (KPI): A quantifiable measure used to evaluate success in meeting objectives.

Process Measure: Indicators that track activities or steps taken toward a goal (e.g., number of workshops conducted).

Outcome Measure: Indicators that measure the final results or impact of an initiative (e.g., percentage increase in knowledge).

Leading Indicator: Metrics that predict future performance or outcomes.

Lagging Indicator: Metrics that show what has already happened or been accomplished.

Dashboard: A visual display of key metrics that provides at-a-glance monitoring of progress.

Discussion Points

  • What makes a KPI meaningful for youth public health initiatives?
  • How do we balance easy-to-measure metrics with truly important ones?
  • When might process measures be more valuable than outcome measures?
  • How can we ensure KPIs capture the full impact of our work on pressure systems?

Activity

Part 1 (15 mins): Learn about different types of KPIs and review examples:

  • Process KPI examples: Number of student-led workshops, percentage of target audience reached, number of resource cards created
  • Outcome KPI examples: Percentage increase in knowledge about resources, number of youth accessing services, self-reported changes in behavior
  • Leading indicator examples: Workshop registration rates, resource material distribution numbers
  • Lagging indicator examples: Post-workshop knowledge retention, service utilization rates

Part 2 (20 mins): Working in your project teams:

  1. Identify 2-3 process KPIs that will track your activities
  2. Develop 2-3 outcome KPIs that will measure impact
  3. Create a simple KPI tracking template that includes:
    • KPI name and definition
    • Data source and collection method
    • Collection frequency
    • Target value
    • Person responsible
  4. Sketch a simple dashboard layout showing how you'll visualize your KPIs

Part 3 (5 mins): Quick gallery walk to view other teams' KPI plans

Reflection Questions

  • Which KPIs will be most meaningful to the youth you're serving?
  • What are the challenges in collecting data for your selected KPIs?
  • How will tracking these measures help you adjust your approach?
  • How can we make data collection and reporting youth-friendly?

Feedback Loops for Public Health 40 mins

  • Designing continuous improvement
  • Reinforcing vs. balancing loops
  • Building adaptive systems
+

Key Terms

Feedback Loop: A system where outputs are routed back as inputs, creating a circuit of cause and effect.

Reinforcing Loop: A cycle where an action produces results that lead to more of the same action (snowball effect).

Balancing Loop: A cycle that seeks equilibrium, where the system self-corrects toward a goal.

Adaptive Management: An approach that incorporates regular feedback to adjust strategies and improve outcomes.

Intervention Point: A place in a system where change can be implemented most effectively.

Discussion Points

  • How are youth health disparities perpetuated through reinforcing loops?
  • What balancing loops might help address the five pressure systems?
  • How can we design our initiative to incorporate regular feedback from participants?
  • What makes some interventions more sustainable than others?

Activity

Part 1 (15 mins): Learn about feedback loops in public health:

  • Review the five pressure systems as examples of reinforcing loops
  • Discuss how interventions can create balancing loops that counteract negative cycles
  • Examine how feedback from program participants can improve program design

Part 2 (20 mins): Working in your project teams:

  1. Identify one harmful reinforcing loop currently affecting youth health in Vallejo related to your project area
  2. Sketch this loop as a simple diagram with arrows showing causation
  3. Design an intervention that creates a balancing loop to counteract the harmful cycle
  4. Develop a specific plan for collecting feedback from youth participants that will help you adjust your intervention over time

Part 3 (5 mins): Teams share their feedback loop diagrams and plans

Reflection Questions

  • How might we collect ongoing feedback from youth participants and community members?
  • What structures can we build to ensure we actually use the feedback we collect?
  • How might transparency about our feedback process build trust with the community?
  • What might prevent a well-designed intervention from creating the balancing loop we intend?
